What factors influence how charitable countries are?



Project Overview

This project explores global trends in charitable giving to try to understand why some countries’ populations are more charitable than others.

There are many factors that might influence how charitable a population is. I chose to explore three – one social (religiosity), one economic (GDP per capita), and one demographic factor (median age).

The analysis performed in this project can be used by charities to understand how best to target fundraising efforts.

The World Giving Index (WGI) score, as measured by Charities Aid Foundation (CAF), is the main variable I use to measure how charitable a country is. CAF produce annual WGI Reports that compile results from a yearly survey conducted by Gallup. In this survey, a random sample of people in around 120 countries are asked whether they have done any of the following in the past month:

Helped a stranger?

Donated money to charity?

Volunteered time?

A country’s WGI score, which is the average percentage of the population who answer yes to these three questions, is therefore determined not by how much people in the country donate, but by whether they donate money or time at all.

Using for-loops in Python, I batch downloaded and merged pdf copies of every available CAF WGI Report. I scraped the required data from these pdfs in a structured format using tabula-py.

I was able to automate much of the process required to obtain this data, but because the tables were read strangely in tabula (probably because they extended over multiple pages), I saved the tabula output as a csv file and had to shift the data into appropriate columns in Excel using vlookups. I then cleaned the csv file in Colab.

Note that none of the charts below use 2019 data as no WGI Report was produced for 2019 due to the pandemic. And although the countries explored in each chart are relatively consistent, a few drop in and out due to data gaps.

Part 2: What factors might explain the differences in charitability between countries?

To see what variables might be driving these patterns in country-level charitability, I merged the CAF data with global data on GDP, religion and median age.

I extracted GDP data from World Bank. This was an ideal source as I could use Python’s wbgapi package to scrape this data, and the dataset updates automatically when new data is released. I scraped data on country-level religiosity and median age from Wikipedia and Worldometers, respectively.

The Kepler map below employs a bivariate colour palette to simultaneously capture two variables. I calculated terciles for the variables, forming nine classifications.

Conclusions

Charitable giving is largely uncorrelated with GDP per capita, religion and median age. This raises questions about what could be driving the trends. Further research could investigate factors like welfare state and wellbeing at a macro- and micro-level.
